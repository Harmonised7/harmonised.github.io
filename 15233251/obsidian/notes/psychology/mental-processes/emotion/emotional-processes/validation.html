validation

validation helps us not only feeling heard, but also feeling seen, which may also come with feeling valued, both by the validating person, and by ourselves - through recognition of our validity via deduction, rather than words.

We seek validation because we have a want and need for recognition and acceptance, to be understood, and confirmation that our feelings are valid.

Unlike rephrasing, validation lets the other person know that you understand why they feel that way, and that you would likely feel that way too in their situation. Which without - would come off as unauthentic, and disconnected.

validation requires empathy. The better your empathy skills, the better your validation skills can become. Not sure what they're feeling? Ask! That's showing literal interest in their feelings.

validation is one of the most powerful social skills, and a basic human need that helps us form connections.

validation is essential for the health of a relationship.

validation can only be achieved without judgment. It is to allow a safe space for a person feeling safe enough to express their feelings and processing them, without labeling them as either either a "Good" feeling, or a "Bad" feeling, because feelings are not good or bad. They are feelings. Some are positive, some are negative and they all serve an important function.

validation is powerful with both positive feelings, and negative feelings, and they are both just as important to be validated!

  • negative feelings
    • helping them feel less crazy by acknowledging their struggles
    • , and providing them a safe space where they can express their feelings without worries and process them more effectively.
  • positive feelings
    • sharing and validating their joy, by letting them know that not only is it valid to have these feelings, but that you also support and encourage them, that you're happy for this person's happiness, further emphasizing the validation.

validation can be used even if you don't agree with the person. Usually to settle things before they get further out of hand (by validating their feelings, and calming their emotions) and hopefully provide an opportunity to share your perspective if applicable and help maintain the relationship.

Effects

effective validation can:

  • calmness:
    • psychology/mental processes/emotion/emotional capacities/feelings/fear
    • frustration
    • arguing
  • Promote:
    • excitement
    • reciprocation
    • connection
  • Assists:
    • acceptance of strong emotion, which allows for resolution via processing
    • Thanks to the newfound clarity: Finding the solution on their own, which is most likely the best solution for them

Examples

  • "I can't even imagine how that feels, that's really rough!!"
  • "I've been through something similar; it really hurts, doesn't it..?"
  • "I absolutely see why this is so difficult on you. It's a really tough situation. It feels really sad, doesn't it?"
  • "I don't know exactly how you feel as I've not been through this myself, and I want to be here for you regardless. It feels really rough, doesn't it."

Pitfalls

  • Only offer a solution after ensurance that the person feels validated, and even then: empathize with the person and determine if it is appropriate in this situation to give advice; Would I want advice in this situation?
    • Chances are high that the person already knows the solution, and only seeks validation.
    • While seeking validation (Whether you're consciously aware that this is what you seek, or are seeking it subconsciously) and receiving a solution instead feels very frustrating, because the person is genuinely trying to help you, and is offering a genuine solution that may help with your situation - one you probably already thought of yourself and deduced why it wouldn't work, or deduced that it would work, yet that's not what feels relevant right now, because what you are truly seeking is validation of your feeling and emotions. What you hoped to receive from this topic is being sneakily shifted away, yet you have no one to accuse of doing anything wrong, because there is only genuinely positive intent.
    • If you want to be extra safe
      • Do not offer a solution at all. That will absolutely stick out in their memories as being genuinely listened to with 0 judgment and without your input as a distraction.
      • By not offering your solution: you never requested anything back.
        • We absolutely do enjoy giving our own input, it feels good to do so, knowing that the person learned something from you, making >you< feel better about yourself and taking away the focus from the person you are comforting.
      • The person experienced no distraction by you from their own feelings and emotions and instead only the validation you provided. It was all for them. This helps them feel important and valued. This will absolutely stick out in their memories and the next time they seek validation from someone else who is less skilled/experienced in validation, when the quality and results of their responsiveness will be put into contrast with yours. That is So bonding and special!
    • If you think the person has been adequately validated and you believe it'd be beneficial and appropriate to give them advice: validate them one more time and ask if they would like to hear your advice.
      • "Damn, that really is difficult. Would you like to hear my opinion?"
      • "Uhh, that's so unfair! Do you want to know what I think?"
      • "Yeah I've been through something like that too and it really is rough. Would you like to hear what I would do?"
        • Even if the person agrees to accept your advice - make sure if they actually want to hear it:
          • People may get socially pressured to accept advice/opinions that they do not want to hear, because it would be perceived as impolite of them. This makes us feel like we have no choice, and may cause negative feelings, such as resentment.
            • "Sure..."
            • "Okay..."
            • -sigh- "Fine..."
            • "Yeah, whatever..."
          • It is up to you to judge the situation and respond accordingly, and decide if you want to continue with sharing your advice or not. In the cases above: The answers were begrudging. You may want to hold back on your advice, unless you think it's really important to share it, like let's say your child really needs to hear this, even if through bitterness. Otherwise, you may instead back down and follow up with something like:
            • "I think that you're in a really tough spot and that really sucks. I sincerely hope you find a way to make it better, and I wish you luck with that."
        • And if instead the response was something more like:
          • "Yeah, I would really appreciate that!"
          • "That'd be great."
          • -sigh- "Yeah, I could use some advice."
            • You're good to go!
        • Of course do not give unsolicited advice if the answer is:
          • "I appreciate the offer and I will pass on it."
          • "No thank you. I just want to forget about it."
          • "Maybe another time, I'm too tired."
            • unsolicitation is rude and unwelcoming. It is disconnecting and invalidating.
      • asking someone if they would like to hear your advice/opinion shows respect, care, and value for their wants, preferences, and boundaries as a person and will most likely be appreciated, even if they reject your offer.
  • Avoid absolutes: expressing to someone that you know "Exactly" how they feel like can be invalidating and disconnecting, because no one truly has the exact same experience. We are all unique, and have come to exist the way we are over millions, if not billions, if not trillions of experiences.
    • absolutes can only be used effectively with people you have a strong bond with.
  • To ensure that your attempt is empathetic: keep your shared experience brief and end it with an invitation for the other person to continue.
    • Without this step, it may come off as sympathetic instead, as the focus/topic of the conversation has now been shifted to you.

Main Elements

To achieve effective validation:

  • Offer it before or instead of offering advice or assurance
  • identification of specific feelings/emotions
  • Offer justification for feeling that feeling/emotion

Extras

Bonus

  • Offer micro validation
    • Super short and quick comments that are designed to validate, not only without shifting/interrupting from the topic, but actually emphasizing it.
      • "What!?"
        • "Yeah!"
      • "No fucking way!!"
        • "Right!?"
      • -exasperated sigh-/-Makes a disapproving face-/-Raises exposed palm to signify disbelief-
        • -feels supported and validated-
