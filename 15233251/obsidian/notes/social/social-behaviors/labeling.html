labeling

socially

  • labeling can be:
    • manipulative
      • labeling can damaging thing to do when stating to someone what/who/how they are, because we are a process, not a result.
        • "You're incompetent/a jerk/a failure!"
          • takes advantage of a recent mistake to attack the person's self-worth by gaslighting them in hopes of damaging it and weakening their resolve, making it easier to manipulate them.
        • "You're so pretty/handsome/kind/smart/rich/strong!"
          • These expressions cause expectations on the person.
            • To themselves:
              • To appear the way they have been labeled in order to not stick out, to not appear as disgenuine, pressuring them to behave in a way that matches the assigned labels.
              • If the person doesn't feel like they are the way they have been labeled:
                • They may gaslight themselves into thinking that something is wrong with them, or that they are insulting the people giving the "compliments", or that the "compliments" are being wasted by them, causing them to feel guilty.
                • Or they my perceive the labels as offensive, rather than complimenting. Whether because they disagree, or don't like to be known/valued for this trait of theirs.
    • constructive
      • However it can be a useful social tool to identify peoples' emotions that they are feeling, but either don't realize they're feeling them, or feel like prioritizing the conversation/you over their feelings. Which is stressful and exhausting.
        • We can recognize this, and express our observations to them, addressing the elephant in the room and dropping the tension to not burden you with their feelings/emotions that they had been experiencing.
          • "You're really anxious right now, aren't you..?"
            • -sigh- "Yeah, this is really important to me..."
              • This recognition takes away the mental energy cost of worrying about burdening you with their own troubles that they didn't find important/urgent enough to share with you, especially if you are currently helping them with something, which would make them feel guilty for requesting even more.
                • You show them that you are paying attention to not only what they have to say, but also their feelings and emotions, showing to them that you care about their current state and wellbeing. And also confirming to them that it indeed is not a burden by choosing to address the elephant in the room, giving them relief
